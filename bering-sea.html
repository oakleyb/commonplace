About Commonplace

Commonplace is a publication of Ecotrust focused on stories of home. Our hope is to create places for connection, for mapping out new understandings of what is common across states, nations, and human nature. We look forward to hearing your stories about the places you live, your experiences of the stories we publish, and your ideas about how to evolve Commonplace in future issues. The Bering Sea is one of the most biologically rich areas on Earth. Nourished by strong upwelling and sea ice movements, the region is the foraging and breeding ground for northern fur seals, gray whales, bearded seals, walrus and eiders, among others.  Native communities have hunted and fished the sea for over 10,000 years, subsisting on the region’s immense natural wealth.  Today, half of American seafood is harvested here.

So what’s the catch?

Fur seal populations are on a 40-year steady decline. Fishing gear has disturbed habitat all over the ocean’s bottom. And, as the gateway to the Arctic, the Bering is vulnerable to a renewed influx of petroleum exploration and mining.

Native people have been on every side of lucrative fishing trades and other resource booms that have colored the sea since Vitus Bering, the Danish admiral in Russian employ first explored it in 1728. Some Native entrepreneurs have found substantial profits. Other local Aleuts have been, literally, slave laborers. Today, though, Native communities are by-and-large watching immense riches flow out of the region, with little to show for it.

In response, Native leaders are framing up an alternative path for sustaining communities and the greater Bering ecosystem.  St. George, a small community in the Pribilof Islands in the center of the Bering, is illustrative: Home to a fur seal rookery, 2.5 million nesting seabirds, and over 200 species of migratory birds, island waters also suffer from heavy fishing by the $1 billion pollock industry. The Native community, led by St. George mayor Pat Pletnikoff is now proposing a cultural heritage marine protected area around the island. It would allow local marine life a chance to replenish and rebuild, and improve the livelihoods and wellbeing of the Native community. 

Native elders elsewhere throughout the Bering have mapped traditional ranges of mammals and other hunting quarry, as well as associated lifeways and traditional knowledge. They are taking stock of what they have, in the face of immense changes, as a first step toward asserting Native rights. 

An upcoming Commonplace issue on the Bering Sea will feature the people and communities throughout the region who are showing a growing awareness about the limits of natural wealth and a sense of urgency about protecting and enhancing what’s left. Nothing less than survival is at stake – survival of a people and the unique place they call home. It’s a story both specific to the Bering and common to us all.
